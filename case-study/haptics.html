In a nutshell

0a

A focused exploration of haptic interaction patterns — the subtle tactile feedback that makes digital interfaces feel physical. From the click wheel on an iPod to modern Taptic Engine responses, I wanted to understand what makes touch feel right.

Why haptics?

0b

We interact with screens hundreds of times a day, but most of those interactions are silent and flat. Haptic feedback adds a layer of communication that bypasses visual attention — a buzz, a click, a pulse can confirm an action, signal a state change, or guide the user without a single pixel changing. This project was about cataloguing those patterns and understanding when, where, and why they work.

The iPod reference

0c

The iPod Classic 4th generation is a masterclass in haptic interaction. The mechanical click wheel provided satisfying physical feedback — each notch, each click, each scroll had a distinct tactile signature. Users could navigate their entire music library without looking at the screen. That's the level of haptic fluency I wanted to explore for modern touch interfaces.

Haptic primitives

1a

I broke down haptic feedback into four core primitives: click (a short, sharp pulse for confirmation), buzz (a sustained rumble for attention), tap (a light, high-frequency nudge for guidance), and pulse (a rhythmic beat for progress or countdown). Each primitive maps to specific interaction contexts.

Pattern catalog

1b

I documented 12 distinct haptic patterns: press confirmation, long-press threshold, swipe boundary, scroll notch, drag snap, toggle switch, slider detent, pull-to-refresh tension, notification hierarchy (silent → tap → buzz), alarm escalation, countdown pulse, and error rejection. Each pattern includes intensity, duration, and frequency parameters.

Scroll & notch feedback

2a

One of the most nuanced haptic challenges is scroll feedback. A continuous smooth scroll feels unnatural — our brains expect friction. I explored magnetic notches (discrete steps with a light tap at each boundary), inertial coasting (a gradual fade of haptic intensity as momentum decays), and end-of-list thud (a low-frequency bump when the scroll boundary is reached).

Toggle & switch haptics

2b

Toggles and switches are prime candidates for haptic enhancement. A toggle that snaps into place with a crisp click feels more reliable than a silent one. I prototyped different haptic profiles for various switch types: a light tap for standard toggles, a firm click for destructive actions, and a springy buzz for three-way switches.

Notification hierarchy

3a

Not all notifications are created equal. I designed a four-tier haptic hierarchy: silent (no vibration, visual only — for low-priority updates), tap (a single light pulse — for social interactions), buzz (a short medium-intensity rumble — for important updates), and alarm (escalating pulses — for urgent alerts). Each tier is distinct enough to be recognised without looking.

Error & rejection feedback

3b

When a user performs an invalid action — tapping a disabled button, swiping where it's not allowed, entering wrong credentials — haptic feedback can communicate the error faster than any visual cue. I explored a short "buzz rejection" pattern: a low-frequency rumble with a sharp attack and quick decay that feels distinctly negative without being jarring.

Key takeaways

4a

Haptic design is still an under-explored territory in most product teams. The same care we put into visual micro-interactions should extend to tactile feedback. A well-designed haptic pattern can reduce cognitive load, improve accessibility, and make interfaces feel more alive. The iPod click wheel got it right 20 years ago — we should be building on that legacy.

Future work: prototyping with actual Taptic Engine hardware, exploring haptic icons (patterned vibrations that convey meaning), and integrating haptic feedback with gesture-based navigation.
